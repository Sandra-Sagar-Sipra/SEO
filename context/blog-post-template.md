## Article Structure

### 1. OPENING (150–200 words)

**Purpose**: Hook the reader. Establish the problem. Promise the solution.

**Opening Hook** (2–3 sentences):
- Start with a provocative statement, surprising statistic, or bold claim
- This is NOT the time for hedging — be direct
- Examples:
  - "85% of AI projects never reach production. Not because of bad technology, but because the wrong problem was chosen."
  - "Most AI vendors will show you an impressive demo. We care more about what happens six months later."
  - "Your AI budget is being wasted on the wrong metrics."

**Problem Statement** (2–3 sentences):
- What challenge is the reader facing?
- Why should they care?
- What's costing them money/time?
- Example: "When you're evaluating AI vendors, the conversation inevitably becomes about features and pricing. But the real question nobody asks is: will this actually reach production? Because if it doesn't, the features don't matter."

**The Promise** (1–2 sentences):
- What will this article teach them?
- What will they be able to do after reading?
- Example: "In this article, we'll show you the five questions to ask any AI vendor before signing. By the end, you'll know exactly which ones are optimising for demos versus production systems."

**Primary Keyword Placement**: Must appear in first 100 words — naturally worked into one of the above sections.

---

### 2. CONTEXT / WHY THIS MATTERS (200–250 words)

**Purpose**: Establish credibility. Show you understand the landscape. Back claims with data.

**What to include**:
- Industry data or research that supports the problem
- Aiquire's track record (if relevant): 40+ production systems, 3.2x ROI, 92% retention
- A specific example or case study setup
- What's at stake if the reader doesn't solve this

**Tone**: Authoritative but conversational. You've seen this pattern before — show it.

**Example**:
"We've delivered 40+ AI projects to production. The ones that shipped fastest and delivered measurable ROI shared something in common: they started with business problems, not technology specs.

The ones that stalled? They started with 'we want to use AI' and worked backwards.

McKinsey research consistently shows 80%+ of enterprise AI projects never reach production. Not because the technology is hard. Because the organisations solving the wrong problem at the wrong scale for the wrong reasons."

---

### 3. MAIN CONTENT (800–1,200 words)

Break into 3–5 main sections. Each section should:
- Be 200–350 words
- Have a clear, specific H2 heading (include keyword variation where natural)
- Include concrete examples, data, or walkthrough steps
- Build logically toward the conclusion

**Structure options**:

**Option A: Step-by-Step How-To**
```
## [H2: How to [achieve outcome] — Step 1]
[200-300 words explaining step 1, with example]

## [H2: Step 2: [specific action]]
[200-300 words with example or data]

## [H2: Step 3: [specific action]]
[200-300 words with example or data]

[Continue with 2–4 more steps as needed]
```

**Option B: Problem-Pattern-Solution**
```
## [H2: The Pattern That Leads to Failure]
[Specific, data-backed explanation of why AI projects fail this way]
[Real example from case study]

## [H2: Why This Happens]
[Root cause analysis — organisational, technical, or process-based]

## [H2: How to Fix It]
[Actionable approach, methodology, or framework]

## [H2: What Success Looks Like]
[Case study results showing the outcome when done right]
```

**Option C: Industry / Concept Breakdown**
```
## [H2: What [Concept] Actually Is]
[Clear definition, no jargon or with jargon clearly explained]

## [H2: Why It Matters]
[Business impact — connect technology to outcomes]

## [H2: Common Mistakes]
[What companies get wrong]

## [H2: How to Get It Right]
[Practical framework or approach]

## [H2: Examples in Practice]
[Real-world applications across industries]
```

**Content Guidelines for Each Section**:
- **Lead with the insight**: Don't build up to it. Put the key point in the first sentence.
- **Use bold** for key concepts or memorable phrases
- **Include examples**: Real scenarios, not abstract theory
- **Data > opinion**: Back claims with numbers, research, or Aiquire's track record
- **Avoid fluff**: Every sentence should move the reader closer to understanding or action
- **Subheadings welcome**: If a section hits 300+ words, break it with an H3

**Formatting Techniques** (use these liberally):
- Bullet points for multiple items (max 5–7 per list)
- Code blocks if demonstrating technical concepts
- Blockquotes for emphasis or to highlight a key insight
- Tables for comparisons or data organisation
- Em dashes — for asides or emphasis
- Numbered lists for sequential steps

### 4. TRANSITION TO APPLICATION (150–200 words)

**Purpose**: Bridge from "here's what's true" to "here's what this means for you."

**What to include**:
- Reflection on what you've just explained
- Why this matters specifically for Aiquire's audience (C-suite, CTOs, ops leaders)
- A subtle setup for the CTA — "If you're facing this problem..."

**Tone**: Still conversational, but starting to gesture toward the call-to-action.

**Example**:
"So here's what we've learned from 40+ production systems: the organisations that get AI right don't start with technology. They start with honesty about what problem they're solving and who it's for. They bring consultants in to push back on assumptions, not just validate them.

If your organisation is at the stage where you're evaluating AI opportunities but want to make sure you're not solving for the wrong thing — that's exactly when an AI strategy engagement makes sense."

## Formatting Checklist

Before submitting for publication:

- [ ] **Opening**: Hooks in first sentence, establishes problem in first 100 words
- [ ] **Primary keyword**: Appears in first 100 words naturally
- [ ] **Headings**: 4–7 main H2 sections; keyword variations in 2–3 of them
- [ ] **Content**: 1,500–2,500 words (shorter only if structure demands it)
- [ ] **Examples**: At least 2–3 concrete examples, scenarios, or case study references
- [ ] **Data**: 3+ specific numbers/stats (with sources cited)
- [ ] **Voice**: Reads like Aiquire — direct, credible, outcome-focused
- [ ] **British English**: organisation, programme, optimise, etc.
- [ ] **Meta title**: 50–60 characters, includes primary keyword
- [ ] **Meta description**: 150–160 characters, includes keyword + CTA
- [ ] **URL slug**: 3–5 words, hyphenated, includes primary keyword
- [ ] **Image alt text**: Describes image, includes keyword naturally, ≤125 chars
- [ ] **Internal links**: 3–5 links to Aiquire service pages, case studies, or related blog posts (with descriptive anchor text)
- [ ] **External links**: 2–3 authority sources cited (with links)
- [ ] **CTA**: Clear, contextual, placed naturally before closing section
- [ ] **Closing section**: Resources or related content included
- [ ] **No hype words**: "Revolutionary", "transformative", "cutting-edge" removed
- [ ] **Passive voice minimised**: Active voice used in 80%+ of sentences
- [ ] **Quality**: Provides genuine insight; not just keyword-stuffed
