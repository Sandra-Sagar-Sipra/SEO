# Aiquire Style Guide

This guide defines writing conventions, formatting standards, and editorial guidelines for all Aiquire blog and marketing content.

---

## Grammar & Mechanics

### English Variant
Aiquire is a UAE-based company serving international clients. **Use British/International English throughout.**

| British (Use) | American (Avoid) |
|---------------|-----------------|
| organisation | organization |
| programme | program (except software programs) |
| optimise | optimize |
| recognise | recognize |
| behaviour | behavior |
| modelled | modeled |
| customisation | customization |
| labour | labor |
| colour | color |
| analyse | analyze |
| realise | realize |
| practise (verb) | practice (verb) |

*Note: "practice" (noun) is the same in both variants.*

### Capitalization

**Headlines & Subheadings**: Sentence case (only first word and proper nouns capitalised)
- ✅ "Why most AI projects fail before they begin"
- ❌ "Why Most AI Projects Fail Before They Begin"

**Product/Service Names**:
- Aiquire (always capitalised, never "aiquire")
- SipraHub (always capitalised)
- Claude (capitalised — it's Anthropic's product name)
- Claude Code (capitalised)
- Claude Cowork (capitalised)
- Double Diamond (capitalised — proper methodology name)
- AI Academy (capitalised as Aiquire's programme name)
- AI Champions Programme (capitalised as named programme)

**Industry Terms**:
- AI (always caps — it's an acronym)
- ML (always caps)
- LLM (always caps)
- NLP (always caps)
- RAG (always caps)
- MLOps (capitalised as written — "L" lowercase)
- API (always caps)
- ROI (always caps)
- KPI (always caps)

### Numbers

**When to spell out**: One through nine
**Use numerals for**: 10 and above, percentages, money, measurements, statistics

- ✅ "three AI projects" / "12 months" / "40+ production systems"
- ✅ "30% reduction" / "$197/month" / "3.2x ROI"
- ✅ "six regional languages" / "four engagement models"

**Large numbers**:
- 1 million (not 1,000,000)
- $5K is acceptable for informal context; $5,000 for formal
- Use commas in numbers ≥ 1,000: 10,000 not 10000

**Stats from Aiquire's track record** (use exactly):
- 40+ AI projects delivered to production
- 3.2x average ROI within 12 months
- 92% client retention rate
- 85% of AI pilots never reach production
- 80%+ of enterprise AI projects never reach production

### Punctuation

**Oxford Comma**: Yes — always use it.
- ✅ "strategy, engineering, and deployment"
- ❌ "strategy, engineering and deployment"

**Em Dashes**: Use — with spaces on both sides — for parenthetical emphasis.
- ✅ "Most AI consultancies stop at the model — we build the full product."
- Format: space + em dash + space

**Hyphens**:
- AI-powered (hyphenated as compound adjective before noun)
- production-grade (hyphenated as compound adjective)
- full-stack (hyphenated)
- real-time (hyphenated as compound adjective)
- end-to-end (hyphenated)
- "AI powered software" when used predicatively (not as adjective): "the software is AI powered"

**Quotation Marks**: Use "double quotes" for quotations and titles.

**Ellipses**: Avoid except for quoting omitted text. Don't use as trailing dots for effect.

**Colon usage**: After a complete sentence introducing a list.
- ✅ "We deliver three things: speed, quality, and ownership."
- ❌ "We deliver: speed, quality, and ownership."

### Abbreviations & Acronyms

**First use**: Spell out on first use, then acronym in parentheses thereafter.
- "Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)" — then just "RAG"
- "machine learning (ML)" — then just "ML"
- Exception: Universally known acronyms (AI, ROI, API, URL, CTA) can be used from first mention.

**Latin abbreviations**: Avoid. Use plain English instead.
- "for example" not "e.g."
- "that is" not "i.e."
- "and so on" not "etc." (or just end the list)

## Word Choice & Usage

### Preferred Terms (Say This → Not That)

| Say This | Not That | Why |
|----------|----------|-----|
| production AI | AI deployment | "Production" is our standard — own the language |
| ship / build / deliver | create, make | Stronger, more engineering-specific verbs |
| business outcome | result, deliverable | More specific to client value |
| organisation | company (when referring to enterprise) | British English + more formal |
| programme | program | British English (unless software) |
| machine learning (ML) | artificial intelligence (AI) | Be precise — use the correct term |
| generative AI | Gen AI (in formal copy) | Spell out in headings and formal contexts |
| return on investment (ROI) | value, savings | ROI is the language the C-suite uses |
| modelled ROI | projected ROI | We model before we build — own that distinction |
| we build | we develop/create | Builders own what they make |
| proof of concept | POC (first mention) | Spell out then abbreviate |
| use case | application, scenario | Industry-standard term |
| hallucination (AI) | "mistake", "error" | Use correct technical term with explanation |

### Words to Avoid

**Hype words** (empty and overused):
- revolutionary, transformative, game-changing
- cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, next-generation
- innovative, pioneering, groundbreaking
- leverage (use "use" instead)
- synergy, synergistic
- holistic, robust (avoid unless genuinely accurate)
- seamlessly, effortlessly

**Vague filler**:
- very, really, truly, actually, basically
- utilize (use "use")
- implement (use "build", "deploy", or "run")
- solution (when used generically — "AI solutions" means nothing)

**Passive voice constructions** (prefer active):
- ❌ "A model was built by our team" → ✅ "Our team built a model"
- ❌ "ROI is delivered" → ✅ "We deliver ROI"

### Inclusive Language
- Gender-neutral language throughout ("they" when pronoun needed)
- "executives" / "leaders" not gendered terms
- Be mindful of idioms that may not translate internationally (Aiquire serves Middle East, Asia, Europe)
- Avoid Western-centric cultural references

## Formatting Standards

### Text Formatting

**Bold**:
- Key concepts, statistics, important takeaways
- Sparingly — if everything is bold, nothing stands out
- ✅ "**3.2x average ROI** within 12 months"
- ✅ "**85% of AI pilots** never reach production"

**Italics**:
- Emphasis where bold would be too heavy
- Titles of external publications
- Technical terms on first use if unusual
- Use sparingly

**Underline**: Never (reserved for links only)

**ALL CAPS**: Only for standard acronyms (AI, ML, LLM, RAG, ROI, MLOps). Never for emphasis.

### Lists

**Bulleted Lists** (use for non-sequential items):
- Capitalise first word of each item
- Period if complete sentence; no punctuation if fragment
- Parallel structure — all sentence fragments OR all complete sentences, not mixed
- Maximum 7 items before splitting into sections

**Numbered Lists** (use for sequential steps, rankings, or ordered priorities):
- Same capitalisation and punctuation rules as bullets
- Ideal for process explanations (Discover → Define → Develop → Deliver)

**Nested Lists**:
- Maximum 2 levels deep
- Use sparingly — if you find yourself nesting deeply, restructure the content

### Links

**Anchor Text**:
- Descriptive and keyword-rich
- ✅ "our AI Strategy consulting practice"
- ✅ "how we reduced resolution time by 30%"
- ❌ "click here"
- ❌ "read more"
- ❌ "learn more" (too generic — add what they'll learn)

**External links**: Open in new tab
**Internal links**: Same tab

### Code & Technical Elements

**Inline code**: Use backticks for file names, model names, technical strings
- `CLAUDE.md`, `FastAPI`, `requirements.txt`

**Technical terms**: Define on first use, then use freely
- "Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) — a technique that grounds LLM outputs in your specific data..."

## Content Structure

### Article Introduction (150–250 words)
1. **Hook** (1–2 sentences): A striking fact, challenge to conventional thinking, or bold claim
   - ✅ "85% of AI projects never reach production. Most fail for the same reason."
   - ✅ "Most AI consultants will show you an impressive demo. We care more about what happens six months later."
2. **Problem** (2–3 sentences): What challenge does this article address?
3. **Promise** (1–2 sentences): What will the reader know or be able to do after reading?
4. **Primary keyword**: Must appear in first 100 words

**Avoid**:
- Starting with "In today's rapidly evolving AI landscape..."
- Starting with "Artificial intelligence is transforming..."
- Weak openings that don't immediately challenge or engage

### Section Length
- **Minimum**: 150 words per H2 section
- **Maximum**: 500 words (break into H3 subsections if longer)
- **Ideal**: 250–350 words per main section

### Conclusion (150–250 words)
1. **Recap** (3–5 bullet points or short paragraph): Key takeaways
2. **So what?**: What should the reader do with this information?
3. **CTA** (1–2 sentences): Contextual call-to-action
   - After educational content: "If you're evaluating where AI belongs in your business, our free discovery call is the fastest way to find out."
   - After case study content: "Ready to see results like this? Book a free discovery call."
   - After service content: "Get a free AI Workflow Audit to identify where we can help."

## SEO-Specific Style

### Meta Titles (50–60 characters)
- Include primary keyword
- Sentence case
- No trailing "| Aiquire" unless space allows
- Benefit-focused or question-based

**Format options**:
- `[Primary Keyword]: [Benefit/Promise]`
- `How to [Goal Without [Common Mistake]`
- `Why [Provocative Claim]: [Context]`

**Examples**:
- ✅ "Why AI projects fail (and how to avoid it)" — 43 chars
- ✅ "MLOps explained: keeping AI alive in production" — 47 chars
- ✅ "AI consulting services: what to look for" — 41 chars
- ❌ "The Ultimate Comprehensive Guide to Everything About AI Strategy for Enterprise" (too long)
- ❌ "AI tips" (too vague)

### Meta Descriptions (150–160 characters)
- Include primary keyword naturally
- Clear value proposition
- End with a CTA phrase
- Complete sentence — never cut off mid-thought

**Formula**: `[Problem/Question]? [Solution]. [Unique angle]. [CTA].`

**Examples**:
- ✅ "85% of AI projects fail before reaching production. Learn why — and how to structure your initiative to be in the 15% that ship. Book a free call." (153 chars)
- ✅ "What is MLOps and why does it matter? Plain-English guide to keeping ML models performing in production, from CI/CD to drift detection." (136 chars)

### URL Slugs
- Lowercase, hyphens between words
- Include primary keyword
- 3–5 words ideal
- No stop words unless necessary

**Format**: `/blog/[primary-keyword-phrase]`

**Examples**:
- ✅ `/blog/why-ai-projects-fail`
- ✅ `/blog/what-is-mlops`
- ✅ `/blog/generative-ai-for-enterprise`
- ❌ `/blog/a-comprehensive-guide-to-understanding-machine-learning-in-2026`

### Alt Text for Images
- Describe what the image shows (accessibility)
- Include keyword naturally where relevant
- Under 125 characters
- No "image of" or "photo of" prefix

## Brand-Specific Guidelines

### Aiquire Product/Service References

**Company references**:
- "Aiquire" — never "the Aiquire platform" (we're not a SaaS platform)
- "SipraHub" — parent company; reference when context requires it
- "our team" — don't say "Aiquire's team" in blog copy (first person is warmer)

**Naming services in copy**:
- AI Strategy & Business Consulting (capitalise as a service name)
- AI/ML Development (with forward slash, no spaces)
- Generative AI & LLM Solutions
- AI-Powered Software Development
- MLOps & AI Infrastructure
- AI Academy (our training brand)
- Packaged AI Solutions
- Claude Training Practice

**Methodology**:
- The Double Diamond (capitalised; explain on first use in any article)
- Discover → Define → Develop → Deliver (four phases; always in this order)

### Competitor References
- Name competitors when relevant for context (Accenture, McKinsey, etc.) — be factual, never disparaging
- Use "other AI consulting firms" as a neutral collective when not naming specifically
- Focus on differentiation ("unlike typical strategy-only consultancies"), not criticism

### Client/Case Study References
- Always use client-approved name and title as in case study
- Don't extrapolate beyond what's in the case study
- Quote marks for direct quotes; paraphrase for general references

## Accessibility

### Screen-Reader Friendly
- Descriptive link text (never "click here")
- Alt text for all images
- Proper heading hierarchy: H1 → H2 → H3, never skip levels
- Table headers labelled clearly

### Plain Language
- 8th–10th grade reading level
- Define technical terms on first use
- Short sentences (avg. 15–20 words)
- Short paragraphs (2–4 sentences)

## Voice & Tone Reminders

### Core Voice Characteristics
1. **Direct**: Get to the point. Don't build up to the insight — lead with it.
2. **Credible**: Back claims with numbers. Show methodology. Reference proof.
3. **Honest**: Say what AI can't do as well as what it can. No empty promises.
4. **Partnering**: "We" with the client — not "we" vs. "them."
5. **Outcome-focused**: Always connect technology to business result.

### Tone by Content Type
- **Educational/how-to**: Instructive, practical, clear — "Here's exactly how..."
- **Thought leadership**: Challenging, confident, evidence-backed — "Most companies get this wrong..."
- **Case study**: Factual, results-first, client-centric — "Here's what happened..."
- **Service pages**: Benefit-first, honest, action-oriented — "Here's what you get..."
